These photos are from spring and early summer 2010. We were still prefabricating sections of the layout in Colorado and transporting them to the final location for installation. We also began to modify some areas of the layout that were installed in 2009 as per our client’s wishes. We pre-assembled many sections that included the cork roadbed up to this point, but we began bringing out more of the layout as generalized pieces as time went on. We standardized the framing knowing that we would be modifying some parts on location. We also began laying the cork on site. I think this approach worked well as the layout became more complex. Our first tests of the Easy DCC system were encouraging and we began to drop the many hundreds of 18 gauge feeder wires and run the 12 gauge buss. Track laying has been an ongoing activity. We have mastered laying Atlas flex track. It is trickier than I thought it would be. There is now over 1000’ of mainline track. We used nearly 1000 3M IDC, (insulation displacement connecters), or “suitcase connectors “ to connect the track feeders to the buss. We started wrapping and soldering these connections and soon realized the project would move ahead much faster if we switched to the 3M connectors. The connections were made in a fraction of the time it takes to solder.  We also installed three turntables: 2 from Millhouse River and one Bowser.

(Left) Here what began as a second level reverse loop has been reconfigured and will assume a new path across the aisle and down the middle above & between Duluth and West Duluth. The difficulty in this new path is what to do as far as access and to hide or scenically blend this upper route with the urban scenery of Duluth. Once it is built; that will be figured out.
(Right) This is looking back towards the entrance with Duluth in the middle right. The ornate stone Chicago Northwestern Depot will be located in the gap adjacent to the passing siding at the end of the two stub sidings. Some of our notations for reassembly of the benchwork are visible. All of the legs are fitted with “Tee Nuts” and carriage bolts to help level the benchwork. We are dealing with a very uneven floor in this building.  At this point the building is beginning to fill with railroad.
(Above) Taken a little later than the preceding photo; the West Virginia yard’s corked tops are installed and connecting sub-roadbed is now being corked by the crew. The building walls were painted blue with a spray of white years ago; this will be covered by Masonite backdrops. The always present plan is in the foreground. Constant checking and rechecking is necessary to make sure everything goes together properly.
(Right) In this photo members of the crew are laying cork roadbed in Virginia. We lay the cork on a thick bead of Liquid Nails which is spread with a notched spreader. The quick tack and waterproof quality of the Liquid Nails made it a good choice for this application. Each section of cork is rolled flat for good adhesion then sanded after the adhesive dries. We lost count as to how many cases of Midwest Cork we’ve gone through!
(Left) This is the benchwork leading into the West Virginia yard. To the far left is the yard at Fort Francis, Ontario which is the largest yard on the layout. While physically close, it will take a train nearly ½ hour at scale speeds to get from West Virginia to Ft. Francis. The benchwork and table tops with cork were prepared in Denver for reassembly on-site.
(Above) The pile of boxes on the left is mostly cases of Atlas flex track.  We had to build from the back of the building to the front in order not to box ourselves in and restrict moving material to the rear of the building. The cart is one of several rolling tool carts. Our intention is to keep tools on the cart at the location we are working. Most of the time the tools end up scattered and usually the one we need at any given moment is 100 feet away at the other end of the building. The boxes hanging from the ceiling are air cleaners. They help keep our dust and dirt in check and keep the air moving.
(Left) The trusty Canadian National GP 9 is testing a newly laid section of mainline. It is drifting past a model of a CN style enclosed water tank. The tank is made from our own resin castings. There will be a number of them throughout the layout. Note the Caboose Industries ground throw on the turnout. Most of the turnouts are set up with ground throws. Some modifications to the turnouts are necessary to make them work. I will write about these mods in a future installment.
(Left) The front of the Roundhouse and the newly installed Millhouse River 24” turntable. The turntable is a wonderful unit with a strong, smooth motor drive and very sturdy bridge and pit. (Right) The underside of the Millhouse turntable showing the mounting tabs, solid construction and big precision drive gear and toothed belt. It took some fiddling to get the bridge track rail even with the yard tracks. We added some shims between the tabs and the plywood and finally got the height set just right. The machine screws are countersunk into the upper surface of the plywood. Nuts and washers allow some adjustment of the turntable pit.
