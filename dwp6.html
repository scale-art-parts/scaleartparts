Spring of 2011 is here and those dreamy months find us back on site working on the DWP. Our goal for the first few trips is the complete the backdrop construction and install the remaining fascia. Additionally we need to begin refining some track and work on switches. We have found the Atlas switches have several deficiencies that are causing problems: 1) the throw bars are flimsy; they do not adapt easily to manual ground throws.  We have started fitting a piece of stripwood under the throwbar to keep it from sagging. 2) the points often stand proud of the closure rails; while this has not caused a lot of derailments, the potential is there. We will be checking every turn out to see if we need to file the points. We have also had to deal with gaps in the rails opening and closing. The building is, unfortunately, not completely tight against outdoor temperature and humidity fluctuations. This has caused some issues that keep us on our toes.

Duluth aisle from the front of the building, (left) and from the rear, (right). We have completed the backdrop/scene divider the separates the West Duluth yard from Duluth proper. The scenery along here will be primarily urban with downtown Duluth represented in the area seen in the right side photo on the right. The tracks under the dark fascia with multiple cutouts are for staging.
These two photos show the end of downtown Duluth, (Left) and the end of the staging tracks, (Right). The duck-under seen in the photo to the left is the only duck-under required while running the mainline. The track seen as the upper level are part of the Cussin subdivision. Trains running counter clockwise, (North) are going to Ft. Francis Ontario. Trains running clockwise, (South) are headed for Virginia, Minnesota and ultimately, Duluth.
Here is the other side of the duck-under.  To the left in the photo is the border town of Ranier, MN. Ranier is home to the customs house as well as the nifty lift bridge that spans the Rainy river which separates the U.S. and Canada. The right side of the aisle is Cook, MN. Once trains round the curve, (in the upper right) they cross over the top of Duluth all the way to the front of the building where they cross the Pratt Truss bridge above the aisle. The trains then run above the staging and arrive here, on the left at Ranier. Having tracks above Duluth are a compromise, even in a building this big there are still challenges regarding space vs. Operation vs. Scenery vs. Accessibility.  For most of the run atop Duluth the trains are hidden.
On the other side of the Duluth divider is West Duluth yard. You can just make out the roundhouse to the right of the photo. On the left is the town of Taft, MN. Once inside this aisle you are completely isolated from the rest of the layout. The effect is to be immersed in the scene. This is obviously a “walk around” layout. Here you follow your train along West Duluth yard and on to the end of the aisle where you must turn and walk back to the point in the photo foreground.
The same aisle less than a month later. Fascia has been installed and the backdrops have been primed. In the far distance the one turn helix with its backdrop and fascia  in place can be seen.
One the prototype DWP the mainline climbed a 1.25%grade out of West Duluth heading North toward the town of Virginia. In the years of steam power this climb required helpers. The line climbed along the face of “The Hill” overlooking West Duluth and crossed two tall bridges and passed through a 520’ tunnel under Ely’s Peak. We rebuilt part of the mainline into this one turn helix to better represent the climb and several of the major features such as the high bridges and the tunnel. The loop represented an interesting challenge as far as scenery. An elliptical backdrop was decided on from which to build the hillside.
In the top right photo: Scott and Aram assemble the structure that will support the backdrop. Care was taken at this stage to insure the supports were plumb and level. We assembled the plywood curves on the floor, all except one connection to allow the loop to be slipped past the support column. In photo 2 you can see the two plywood curve formers that define the shape of the backdrop. It does not follow the curvature of the track exactly. We were careful to align the two formers one above the other.  
1X3 lumber and curved supports cut from 3/4” plywood have been assembled in photos 2 and 3.  Photo 2 is taken from the outside aisle while photo 3 is the “backside”.  Though the structure looks rickety, it is really very strong. Once the Masonite is attached it will be even stronger.
Further along in photo 4, the Masonite has been attached to the framing. This was a fairly tight bend though it does not over-stress the hardboard. We have been using Carpenter’s Glue and counter-sunk drywall screws to attach the hardboard to the framing.
Photo 5 shows the “backside” again. Note that the backdrop extends lower on this side. This is so the hill can make a descent in elevation. In reality this portion of the railroad is a fairly straight line from South to North, but to fit our plan it has to make a 360 degree loop. As the track loops; so must the scenery.
In photo 6 The Masonite is primed and the seams taped and “mudded”. We use drywall materials for blending the sheets of hardboard together. The fascia is also in place which give definition to the scene. It is obvious here that the hillside will be steep. This end will feature the tunnel which will have to be greatly compressed as in O scale it would be nearly 10 feet long if we modeled it full to scale. Our tunnel will be about 4’ to 4 1/2’ long. Note the top of the Easy DCC command station. This is located on the wye in the foreground. This is Simar where the helper engines were watered and turned for the trip back down to West Duluth.
Photo 7 is the location of one of the bridges located on the loop. This will be a tall girder trestle which on the prototype spanned Stewart creek. More on the installation of this and the other bridge will follow in the months ahead.
