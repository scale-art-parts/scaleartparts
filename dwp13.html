The locomotive is a Weaver 2-8-0 converted to represent DWP # 1984, a Schenectady Consolidation. Key appearance changes include: boiler top check valve and new piping, bell move to front of boiler, new handrails, (loco and tender), shroud around the whistle, new coal bunker, new crew figures from Artista, paint, my own Alps printed decals and light weathering.

The loco has a MDC large scale DCC decoder with steam sound. It carries two speakers; a big one in the tender and a smaller one facing up to the open stack.  I’m pleased with the MDC decoder; the sound is good and there are plenty of bells and whistles to choose from. There are also a number of chuffs, chuff rates and other effects to program. The board is a bit large, but in this case it fits in the boiler. It is also reasonably priced when compared to some of the more sophisticated decoder such as QSI. The locomotive runs quite well and as yet is not really broken-in.

Photos from the era being modeled; mid-1950's, show the loco to be relatively clean with a shiny boiler jacket, heavily sooted smoke box and obvious backwash down the sides of the boiler in front of the cab from the generator and auxiliary dome. I tried to replicate this look as seen in the photo below. I used a semi-gloss finish instead of gloss to avoid a toy-like finish.

While not a perfect replica of the prototype, I believe it looks the part.

In 1948 the DWP replaced it’s old wooden coaling tower at West Duluth with this new pre-fabricated coaling plant. I have not found out for certain the makers of these towers, but some say Fairbanks-Morse was the manufacturer. Research indicates that these plants utilized the Redler “en-mass” conveyor/elevator system to move coal from the dump to the steel bin. The Redler system was invented in the 1920’s and the company is still in buisness, http://www.redler.com/en/aboutus/the-en-masse-principal/ The Canadians called these “Redler 50 ton Coal plants. It is possible that Fairbanks-Morse manufactured the tower while employing the Redler system for coal handling. These plant seem to have been more common on Canadian roads than on US railroads, but as the PEG was Canadian National owned it makes sense that this type of coaling plant would appear on the DWP.  Any photos I’ve found show hoppers spotted over the coal dump. A similar tower was located at the West Virginia yard as well.

The first step in making a model of this structure was to draw a scale plan, interpreting what was seen in the various photos. By studying the photos most of the key features can be discerned.  Determining the size requires finding an object where the size is known or can be fairly assumed. In the case of this model there was a man standing on the tender which gave some relative scaling. Mostly the scaling of individual features is “educated” guess work. The tilt of the can was unknown, but study of the photos gave an angle that looked right. I think the final drawing is pretty close and most important has the character and feel of the prototype.

My model was constructed from mostly basic scratch-building materials: strip and sheet styrene, plaststruct structural shapes, (mostly angle) & model plywood for the base. I used a section of acrylic tube for the can. Its expensive stuff, about $20.00/foot. The acrylic tube was cut to length then chucked on a lathe. Three channels were cut into the tube for the welds/seams which you can see as white bands in the photo. The ends were made by cementing sheet styrene to the ends of the tube then cutting and sanding the ends to size once the cement had cured.

The tricky part was setting the angle of the can. Once the angle was determined the Plastruct ABS angle was cut. The support was built up before cementing the can in place. All the bracing is Plastruct angle.

The chute and chute supports were built up from styrene. Originally it was planned to allow the chute to pivot, but as there were no clear photos of the coal door or release mechanism I decided the fix it in place. The upper level platform was another screwy thing to build due to the tilt of the can. It was built up from Plastruct angle and styrene plus one of my electric motor castings. The conveyor and ladder on the back of the unit are styrene and Plastruct.

The base is built from model aircraft plywood and some Plastruct I beams to carry the rails over the coal dump. The track is a section of Atlas flex with the ties removed over the pit. A hole the size of the pit will be cut out of the yard and the unit will slide down into place in alignment with the existing yard tracks.  

Looking at the coal dump: I decided to put a kind of “Grizzly”in place. To those unfamiliar with the term it is used in mining and milling to prevent over large material from passing through. In this case the thought was to prevent careless personnel from falling into the dump. The grizzly is made from code 80 rail. I don’t know if this is common practice, but it made sense to me. Once in place, the code 148 Atlas track should line up perfectly with the track already laid in the yard. More on that after we get on site.

A close up of the styrene chute. Small brass eyebolts were formed for the lines that attach to the chute. As near as I can tell; the chute is first pulled down by a line that attaches the the flap in the front. This brings two other lines into reach which when pulled, draw down the long levers which open the coal door. The chute is also counter-weighted.

Two views of the upper platform show the bits that went into making the top of the coal conveyor. Here again there were no clear photos. The motor is my own resin piece as is the belt drive guard on the side. The railing is made of Plastruct angle. I don’t know what the “thing” is below the platform, but it can be seen in photos so there it is.

What a difference a little paint makes! A few Tichy lamps were added along with the ropes to dump the coal. The entire structure was primed with gray auto primer. This was followed with various layers of black. The black layers ranged from a very dark gray to very dark black. The tower was lightly streaked with rust and light gray. Overall it is intended to like like something that is perpetually coated in a fine layer of coal dust.

To improve the performance of the Weaver loco the tender was converted for power pick up. All four axles pick up juice and send it to the DCC decoder in the loco. The blackening was removed from the wheel backs and treads. Phosphor bronze wire was bent to shape and soldered to small pads of circuit board material. The circuit board pieces are epoxied to the trucks with some heavy paper insulation. The original Weaver harness is used to conduct the power to the loco and for the speaker hook up as well. The locomotive’s performance is greatly improved, particularly over the #7.5 Atlas switches.
