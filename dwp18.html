Time to focus on a few structures needed on the DWP. First up is the ornate depot located in Virginia Minnesota. Virginia is about halfway between Duluth and Ft. Francis. Virginia is where the Missabe interchanges with the DWP and is a busy area for iron ore mining. In 1913 the DWP completed it’s own depot in Virginia. Up to this point the DWP used the Duluth, Missabe & Northern depot. The new depot was built of brick and sandstone. The interesting thing is that the depot was built on pilings at the edge of Silver Lake. A single track served the depot and it was built on a trestle as part of the “loop line” that diverted from the DWP mainline which continued to the West Virginia yard, to downtown Virginia. This loop line also interchanged with the Missabe at Rainy Junction. The depot still stands today, but is now used as a bank.

This is the view of the depot as it will be seen on the layout.  In effect, the viewer will be standing in Silver Lake, famous for the World’s largest loon decoy, which float out in the lake in the summer months. In this photo the trestle can still be seen although there is no rail on it. The trim of the building is painted white although the few color photos I’ve seen from the 50’s show the trim to be green like other Canadian National structures. The fire escape is also a contemporary addition which was not in evidence during the depots active years. The third storey was known as the “crows nest” and was home to the dispatcher.

The initial test fitting showed that most all of the parts fit together quite nicely. There were a few adjustments and some changes, but overall our first cutting worked out for us.

The first step for us in building this model was to create working scale drawings. We were unable to locate any builder’s drawings or other documentation. We were also not prepared to make a field trip to Virginia to make measurements so photos were used to make the drawings. When scaling from photos it is helpful if a view can be found that is close to straight on. We were lucky enough to find one that had workers building the depot in the book Delivered with Pride by Jon A. Severson. This made scaling fairly easy and I believe were are very close. Our drawings are done in Coreldraw.

The scale drawings were deconstructed and prepared for the laser cutter. We decided to cut the parts from 1/8” thick white acrylic sheet. Some of the sheets were pre-painted prior to cutting, others were not. The brick pattern was etched into the sheet. The window and door openings were cut out at the same time is the parts were cut.

The doors and windows are cut from 1/32” thick birch plywood. They are built up from 4-5 parts for dimension and depth.

Other parts such as the towers and sandstone trim are cast  resin from hand carved patterns.

Here one of the corner towers is being test fit to see where the walls will need to be notched. We ended up notching and beveling the walls for a close fit.

Windows and door were test fit as well. Here is where the laser really pays for itself. Note the arch detail above the window and door openings. The wall is laying on top of the drawing so the photo looks a little odd. The door is unpainted birch plywood. There are etched locations for the eve brackets which appear as faint lines in the photo.

The center section after cementing together. The swell thing about acrylic is that it cements with solvent cement like styrene for strong joints. Small 45 degree triangles are used to help square things up as well as add some strength to the corners. Because resin parts can be tricky to adhere to dissimilar materials and owing to the fact that these are heavy castings with small attachment points; some mechanical fasteners were used to make certain they will not get knocked off the building.

The windows and doors are gang primed and painted prior to glazing and installation. Although the trim is now white, color photos form the 50’s in “Duluth, Winnipeg & Pacific in color” by David C. Schauer show the depot having Green trim.

Here is the depot mostly assembled. The center section was painted assembled while the freight sections were painted prior to assembly. The windows are installed with glazing and shades. Some of the sandstone trim is installed. Some putty was required so there will be touch up painting.

Here is a closer look at the center section. The towers turned out looking very nice. There will be a little detail inside to view through the windows. Lighting is also being installed.

Finally, one last look with a mock up of the peaked roof in place. These parts are laser cut from matte board with lines for the shingle engraved. Soon the depot will be finished along with the platform. The trestle is completed and ready to install on the layout. We are very pleased with how quickly we were able to make the parts and assemble them into this great model. Next up is the C&NW depot in downtown Duluth, which the DWP used.
