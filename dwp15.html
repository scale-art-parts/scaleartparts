April 2012: Time to work on the Hill and tunnel in earnest. The fascia was re-done. We made it higher with contours for the rock outcrops to come. A view port was cut into the fascia to allow viewing and easy access to the inside of the tunnel. Plaster cloth hardshell was worked in over the existing expanding foam hardshell completed late last year. A new backdrop was constructed in the reverse loop at Duluth. Basic ground cover and ballasting continue. John Crawford joined us for our April work session. He did most of the ballast.  

Here is a look at the opening in the fascia. An access hatch with a door was originally planned, but this idea took hold.It was simpler and provided an interesting location for viewing trains underground. The tracks are about 12” in from the fascia so a little bit of blasted rock was crafted to frame the opening. Using this opening it is possible to reach all of the otherwise hidden track for cleaning.

Above the opening is some of the plaster cloth hardshell. Scott like to work a lot of the irregular shapes that define the mountain into the hardshell in preparation for rock castings. Naturally, we make a mess doing this, but plaster cleans up pretty easy and if you are thinking a head, drop cloths save a lot of cleanup time.  

The reconfigured tunnel opening looks more true to the prototype location. The  real tunnel is still accessible and is on a hiking trail above West Duluth. The plaster cloth does not stick to the foam sheets, but enough plaster was laid on the make it stay in place. Some cardboard strips and news paper were used to create new shapes on top of the foam sheets. It is important to check clearances for trains. One must also keep in mind that the next layer; the rock castings add a fair amount of thickness to these areas.

A few more shots of the newly hardshelled hill. There are a least two layers of plaster cloth and this is allowed to dry for several days before castings are added. In our case, the casting will come about a month later. We have found sources for remnants of plaster cloth on the Internet. It is sold in 20 pound lots for far less that the rolls at craft and hobby stores. The stuff is good, but can be a bit ragged. Scott like to brush a layer of plaster onto the plaster cloth surface to add a little more strength and so the surface does not weaken when wet castings are applied.    

We only made one trip in April, so not as much progress was made as most months when we make two trips to the project site. Lots of smaller things were done such as wiring in an auto reverse module for the Millhouse River turntable at West Duluth yard. A Digitrax unit is used and works great. We no longer need to throw a DPDT switch when a loco is turned.

The Fallen Barn was finished and installed in place. The shingles and debris add a lot to the kit which is made by Laser-Art, Branchline Trains. Scott was skeptical of the outcome when the kit contents were first viewed, but the final result looks pretty convincing. We are not sure what disaster befell this poor structure, but it is in definite need of rehab. The tractors are from Athern, I think. Not the old rusted tractor next to the barn. The trees are Super Trees from Scenic Express. They are a do it yourself tree; the foliage must be bought add applied by the modeler. The cardboard tube is a stand-in for a silo. A scratch-built silo is in the works.

The two photos above demonstrate one technique used to camouflage the optical detection sensors used to activate the crossing signals further down the line.  The photo on the left shows the location of the two sensors; Infrared LED is on the right side of the track and the phototransistor is on the left side. The phototransistor is hidden behind a short section of fence with bushes and debris to help hide it. The infrared LED is inside a rusty barrel, see the close-up photo on the right.

More detail has been added to the hobo camp, including more foliage, a rusted panel truck and a flickering campfire. Electronics for the campfire come from Evan’s designs and uses LEDs. I’ll post better photos of the campfire soon.

Our first work session in May resulted in finishing the rockwork around the tunnel on the hill. We also continued to put down ground cover and worked on new areas with hardshell. I forgot the compact flash card for my camera this trip and we could not find one locally to buy that my camera would recognize. So there are few photos, taken with a cell phone and the quality is not so great, but here are some images of the hill, etc. I’ll take more shots next trip.

The first task is casting the rock faces in molding plaster. Scott prefers molding plaster over Hydrocal as it takes color better., but is still much harder than plaster of Paris. John and Scott spent one entire day casting the rocks from numerous different molds. Some carving is also done to blend the differing casting into one another. We are aiming for a basalt structure with lots of vertical uplift and fracturing.

The casting dried sufficiently overnight to allow the coloring process to begin. Diluted artist acrylic paints are “slopped” over the castings. Scott works with few colors: Raw Sienna, Burnt Sienna & Burnt Umber are the three base colors. They are applied randomly and a spray bottle of clear water is used to keep the colors wet and moving while being brushed on the rocks. Some raw plaster is allowed to remain through this process.

The big changes appear when a layer of diluted black acrylic paint is brushed over the preceding three colors and worked into all the detail and crevasses. Again, clear water is sprayed to keep the black tint moving. The result at this point can be rather dark and not so nice to look at, but there is more work to be done that changes all that. Once the black wash is dry all of the previously used colors plus white and Gray are squeezed from the tube onto a pallet. All of these colors are dry brushed onto the rock surface to blend and highlight. While the rocks in the real life location we are modeling are predominately Gray, there is always some variation of color and this is the look we are going for. We want some hints of brown and tan as well as a little redish hue here and there.

Once the colors are satisfactory ground cover can be added. This step really make the previous work “pop”.

This over view shows a variety of colors and textures in the ground cover. Most of the ground foam is from Timberline Scenery.

Pleas excuse the fuzziness of the photos. While the phone camera is pretty good, it lacks depth of field. Two final shots of the finished rock and surrounding scenery. There are still scores of trees to add to this area so there will be more photos to come.
