Our friend Jeff MacDonald worked for a week painting backdrops. The results truly have altered the appearance and broadened our horizons! Below is the Laurentian Divider with painted sky and a new fascia.  Photo taken August 2011.
Some various shots taken August 2011: Top left, Ranier Minnesota on the left side of the aisle, Cook, Minnesota on the right. Top right, looks over the West Virginia roundhouse toward the Laurentian Divide, Ft. Francis Ontario is to the left of the photo. Lower left is West Duluth with some of the buildings being placed. Lower right, West Duluth yard looking toward the “Hill”.
“The Hill” Elys Peak has the support structure for the hard-shell to come. Just visible inside the web is the tunnel. It ended up being just over four feet long. The tunnel liner is blue insulation board that has been carved and painted to represent blasted rock. The middle section will have an access hatch built in for cleaning the track. Scenery formers also line the right side against the backdrop. We are cutting these former from 1” thick expanded foam insulation board.
Another look at those scenery formers, (or ribs) that will support the expanded foam hard-shell. We decided to use the type of scenery developed by Bragdon Enterprises, “Geodesic Scenery”. http://www.bragdonent.com/
 While we are not concerned about weight, we are wanting to move fast and be as clean as possible. This approach give us speed and relative neatness. We do end up with a lot of foam scrap and such, but that is easier to clean up than plaster dust and splatters.
Lower left photo, There was some left over bits of hard-shell left over from some previous scenery so it was used around this stone arch bridge. Everything is hot glued together so the one annoying by product is the string from the hot glue. It gets everywhere! Watch out for the hot glue itself; we are using a high wattage industrial glue gun and the glue is ultra hot. We wear cheap mechanic type gloves found at Harbor Freight and long sleeves. Scott still managed to get a couple of nasty burns when his attention was diverted. In spite of all that, the scenery goes quickly and the strings can be melted with a hot air gun.
Below: Three views of the Stewart Creek trestle on the south approach to the tunnel. It is made from the resin girders sold by Scenic Express. The abutments are castings made in our own shops. The deck is wooden bridge ties with hand-spiked rail. Lower left photo: the plate girder bridge that carries the loop over itself is seen in the distance.
Above Left: CN GP-9 #4307 is swallowed by the South portal of the tunnel under Ely’s Peak. Middle: the outside of the tunnel liner under the scenery formers. Above Right: CN GP-9 #4307 emerging through the North portal. This area will feature lots of rock castings and vertical scenery. It is the only location on the railroad to feature rugged, rocky terrain.  
North of the tunnel is the Koki Creek bridge. Similar to Stewart Creek except for a cast concrete pillar instead of steel trestle bents.
