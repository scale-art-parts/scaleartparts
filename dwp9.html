It’s hard to know where to begin this page, so much is going on. Once the sky background was painted by Jeff on all the backdrops we could move ahead with forming up the scenery. As stated on the previous page we are using expanded urethane foam as the hardshell. We are primarily using 1” thick expanded urethane insulation board to give shape to the hills. We are using high heat glue guns to hold the scenery together, so we use very little of the blue insulation board as the glue simply melts it. In places where we do use it we have to go with a low temp type glue. We use the high temp glue because it is faster to work with.  In addition to forming up scenery and applying hardshell; John Crawford has joined the team and has been painting the rails and cross ties for a more realistic right of way. We have also purchased large quantities of ground foam from Timberline Scenery. Some of this material has been applied as later photos show. Timberline is also supplying a large portion of the Trees that are needed for the layout.
Within the “Hill Loop” we had an over/under situation. The upper track is carried on an Atlas plate girder bridge that has had the deck modified to accommodate a curve. The 1” thick foam board formers are clearly shown. They are hot glued to each other and the bench work. Profiles are cut with serrated steak knives. Some of the pieces have had the metallic skin removed; it is not important to have the skin on or off as it peels off easily it often come off while cutting the piece.
We had to scab in a piece of Masonite behind the Stewart Creek bridge; call it poor planning if you will, but the scene drops down lower than originally conceived. This type of area is rather tricky to form up. In the upper right the foam board ribs are crisscrossed with drywall shims to help support the hardshell to come.
At the South tunnel portal there is blue board, foam board and drywall shims combined.
The hill over the tunnel has been covered with several sheets of hardshell. It is important that the sheets be well attached to the supporting structure. We use high temperature hot glue to allow time to get everything down tight. Seams between sheets will be blended with acrylic caulking.
Here at the North portal, hardshell has been attached to the blue board. It looks kind of ugly, but once cured, this type of hardshell is tough, self-supporting and light weight. In this case the light-weight aspect is not important, but the other qualities are. Two major benefits are that this is fast to put down and clean with no dust.
Here are some of the tools of the hardshell trade; high temp glue guns. Our guns are 80 watt, but we are upgrading to 300 watt guns as they will melt the glue sticks faster. Sometimes we find ourselves waiting on the glue to melt in the gun so we can move on. We use a heat gun to help form the hardshell sheets and re-melt the glue if we wish to make a change. The heat gun is also handy in making the glue strings disappear.
Here Noah is attaching hardshell to one of the bridge abutments at Koki Creek. Note he is wearing gloves to protect his hands from the hot glue. The stuff give nasty burns!!!! It is nearly impossible to keep your finger out of the hot glue, but we have found these inexpensive gloves from Harbor Freight keep our fingers safe. While the harden glue can be picked off, eventually the gloves are trashed and a new pair quickly broken in. It is not a bad idea to wear a long sleeved shirt as well. You never know where a glob of the hot glue may end up.  
Here is a close up look at the hardshell completed around a bridge abutment. These abutments are Scale Art Parts resin castings. They are designed to work with the girders and bridge feet sold by Scenic Express. The next step in this location is to paint the hardshell with flat latex paint; an earth color which is followed with ground cover.
These two photos show the area behind the West Duluth yard. Some additional painting has been done to the backdrop to help pull the backdrop and the 3D scenery together. The hardshell is complete and some scenery has been completed down near the roundhouse. The blue strip is some foamboard that has been laid as a base for some yard structure yet to come. A couple of mobile homes have been worked into the background and a farmhouse is being checked for possible inclusion into the scene.
These two trailers are from Plasticville and have been re-worked to give them a little more realistic appearance.
