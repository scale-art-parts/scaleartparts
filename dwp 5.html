Time marches on and progress on the O scale Duluth Winnipeg & Pacific continues. While the last of the benchwork is assembled and track is laid, wiring moves ahead with a will. As stated before; we are using EasyDCC made by CVP Products out of Richardson, Texas; www.cvpusa.com. The system is straight forward and modular which makes installation a snap. We determined to divide the layout into 10 isolated zones each with it’s own “Zonemaster” as the boosters/circuit breakers are called in the EasyDCC system. Zonemasters with auto-reverse are used for the two reverse loops. The power buss is 12 gauge stranded wire while the drops, (feeders) are 18 gauge stranded. Drops are made on every section of flex for reliable power. Each Zonemaster has its own 15 volt/7 amp power supply. The command signal runs from the centrally located base station to each Zonemaster via  6 conductor telephone cables with modular plugs into each unit. The handheld units are radio transmitters so there are no plug-in locations needed on the fascia. Our early test were very encouraging as we were able to control a decoder equipped loco from any location in the building.
(Left) A Weaver 2-8-0 with an MRC sound decoder runs the first test of the auto-reverse loop. After testing several speeds it was decided that the trigger sections in the rail should be moved further from the turnout. The turnout is automated to flip the the leg of the approaching train. This is done by combining a Tortoise switch motor with a “Hare” from DCC Specialties. The Hare is a circuit board the snap on the Tortoise contacts and is wired to trigger sections of rail that detect the approaching train and throws the switch accordingly. It works great!! Both the reverse loops are entirely “hands-off” operations.
The turnouts can be thrown manually with the addition of a momentary push button.
(Right) This area is near the entrance to Virginia. We installed an Old Pullman crossing in the midst of all this trackage and wanted to be able to run through it without throwing DPDT switches to change the polarity of the frogs. We found just the circuit from Tam Valley Depot, www.tamvalleydepot.com. They manufacture the “Hex Frog Juicer” that performs the function flawlessly. Trains run through this section of track as if it was nothing other than a simple straight section. It is a terrific product that does just what the manufacturer says it will do.
By the fall of 2010 all the benchwork is complete. Virtually all the track is laid, the wiring is finished and trains are running the entire layout. We are excited and spend part of a day playing with trains. We found it takes a train 1 hour to run from the first reverse loop around the system and back to it’s starting point at a scale 40 mph. This is actually too fast for a number of sections on the prototype. Add to this switching, helpers the whole bit and there is plenty of railroading fun to be had! By fall we have also begun to erect scene dividers and backdrops as well as adding fascia to the benchwork. Before our winter hiatus, we don’t generally plan trips in the winter, Some small amount of scenery was begun near the entrance. Things are looking good at this point.
(Left) An overall view from the rear of the building. To the far left the upper level track is visible as it runs above West Duluth. The overhead beams and supports have been painted white, (they were red and Gray), which profoundly improved the appearance of the building. We are looking inside a loop that climbs out of West Virginia through the Laurentian Divide and eventually hooks into that elevated trackage above Duluth. The grade is kept to 1% and less.
(Right) Looking from the other side into Virginia the elevated track is more obvious. The primed backdrop is seen beyond and a little of the painted backdrop can be seen in the far upper left.
(Above Left) This is all that remains visible of one reverse loop. The backdrop cuts across the center. The openings will be disguised with buildings and overhead details. In the far distance the hardshell is evident above the backdrop and fascia.
(Above Right) The climb out of West Duluth heading North is along a mountainside and through a tunnel. We were never satisfied with the original design for this and after all the other benchwork was built we could see there was room for a one turn helix around this post. The result will be more dramatic than the original design.
(Above Left) A close up look at the “Hex Frog Juicer” as installed. Note how simple the wiring is: red and black from the DCC buss; blue and yellow to the xing frog. (Above Right) The xing with the juicer underneath. Note the IDC, (suitcase) connectors attaching feeders to the buss.  
(Right) A slightly modified Atlas Truss Bridge spans the entrance to the Duluth aisle. This is a “Nod under” as the tracks are a little over 5’ off the floor here. The bridge is removable to make moving around during construction easier. It should not need to be removed during normal operations. The two abutments are made from flat resin sheets of our own patterns
 & molds. The sheets are made to look like formed concrete.
The left and right abutments for the Atlas bridge. Note the board detail on the flat sides. There is a strip of copper circuit board on each end where the bridge rests on the abutment. These power the bridge track. The scenery here is basic plaster hardshell with ground foam and a couple of small plaster rock castings. The scenery process will be discussed in greater detail as that phase of the layout is worked on.
(Left) The DWP crosses the Rainy River between Ranier MN. And Ft. Francis, Ontario. The real drawbridge is a Schirtzer rolling bridge. We decided to rework an old Lionel drawbridge to avoid a long and arduous scratch-building project. While somewhat too short, it doesn’t look too bad. The AC motor was replaced with a quieter DC unit. It works well.
A unique set of plunger type contacts were machined to carry DCC power to the bridge track (above right). The bridge itself has
 a couple of pieces of circuit board material as seen on the left. The piers and abutments are made of wood with a custom
printed concrete image. The scenery at this location is roughed-in with blue foam board.
