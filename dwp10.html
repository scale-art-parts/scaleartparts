This photo illustrates the several steps in scenery: formers, hardshell and basic ground covers. A variety of colors and textures are put down and an effort is made to keep the colors consistent between the backdrop image and the 3D scenery.
The area around the West Duluth roundhouse became the first major area to receive scenery. We left a gaping hole behind the roundhouse to provide access to the building. As you can see the roof is not in place yet nor is it detailed. These are issues soon to be rectified.
Looking over the turntable the roof of a distant farmhouse is glimpsed. This is an HO structure placed low and well behind the O scale structure for some forced perspective.
There is actually a little HO farm behind the hills. This is a view obtainable only with a short ladder. In putting this scene together it was decided that painting additional scenes on the sky were necessary to keep the farm from “floating” in the air.
This would be a more typical view.
Here is John painting track. There is a lot of track to paint and John has been a real sport about getting the job done. He is using a trigger type airbrush with which he is applying PolyScale grimy black to the sides of the rail. This is followed by a auto detailing airgun filled with PolyScale rail tie brown. The brown is directed at the cross ties, but the overspray also tones down the black and gives our track a nice weathered look.
Before and after painting. The difference is striking. Once the track is ballasted we will have a very presentable right of way.
I find it interesting that Millhouse River Studios offers no suggestions regarding controls for their turntables. One could choose to go with a small model railroad power pack, but that is no good if you want a panel mounted control or something more elegant. I searched the Internet looking for DC motor control that had two directions and very fine speed control. I found this controller at Carl’s Electronics . This controller is model #CANUK-1125; a 7A Bidirectional DC motor speed controller. It comes pre-built for about $40.00. I’m very pleased with the fine control. I made a clear acrylic faceplate and mount for the circuit board with a printed graphic to show direction and stop position. The knob is from Radio Shack.
The fascia near the turntable at West Virginia Yard lacked a good location for a panel mounted control that would be at a convenient height. I decided to mount the control in one end of a small yard building at the edge of the bench work. A small structure was designed around the circuit board/faceplate assembly and built from scratch. The brick siding is Plastruct, the door and windows are our own castings available through Scale Art Parts. There are still a few bits to add to the building once the controller is “permanently” mounted.
An inside view shows the robust framing of this little building. It was over-built since it houses the controller that will be used frequently to rotate the turntable.
The “rear” view shows the big double door. Note the weathered painted brick and the clapboard gables. There will be a ramp leading into this end and lots of appropriate clutter around the building once it is in place on the layout. More photos will be posted of the final placement...stay tuned.
