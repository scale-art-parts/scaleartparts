March 19 2012: It has been 35 months since we screwed the first pieces of wood together for this project. The progress has been consistent with only an occasional delay or reversal to make a change. The  layout is really beginning to take on a personality now that more scenery is being added. The Easy DCC system has proven to be reliable even after several months of being idle.  The layout has stabilized and we see no new shifting of the track due to expansion and contraction of the roadbed. This spring will see many new element added to the layout as scenery proceeds and new hardshell is laid in place. We will be making a few changes to some areas such as: additional track and rerouting some track for the Richeleau Taconite plant, added track for a paper mill at Ft. Francis and a couple of freight/warehouse tracks in front of the Duluth depot.  We also are redoing the backdrop/fascia in the reverse loop East of Duluth and at the tunnel on Elys Peak. We have also decided to re-hardshell Elys peak in plaster cloth and use plaster rock castings.  We find the expanded foam hardshell to be fast and very suitable for rolling hills and flats, but are not too happy with using it for rocky mountainsides and our experimentation with the cast resin rock faces have been disappointing. So, make to the materials we know and love, good old plaster.

Many structures remain to be built, both kit and scratch-built. There are also many pre-made structures stockpiled such as MTH, Lionel, Woodland Scenics and others, but all of them need some attention before they get placed. We still need thousands of trees, both conifer and deciduous types. Locomotives require DCC installs and cosmetic changes. We have lots to do and I will continue to chronicle as much as possible as we progress.   

Noah and I had a great first on-site work session of 2012. After a couple of hours of regaining our bearings and organizing the tools and materials we dove right into the work at hand. Noah started making and placing sheets of expanded foam hard shell to fill open areas along the South wall. Scott went to work on the rural scene on the end of the second peninsula leading into Virginia. The work began by installing a pair of Tomar crossing signals and wiring them to detection, sound and light circuits from Azatrax, http://www.azatrax.com/ .  Azatrax uses an infrared detection system that works very well. Note in the photo; the brake lights on the Ford waiting at the xing. There is a location on the control board that provides a simple on for such things as a tip light on a crossing gate. I decided to use it to illuminate two red LEDs that I installed in the read fender of the model car. I think its pretty cool. The roadway surface is the foam core from “FOAMCORE board” with the paper removed. It is painted with acrylics after being glued to the layout. We had a camera malfunction so I did not get as many shots of the various processes and elements as I wanted. I will get into more detail on the Xing electronics and the scene, including the roadway in a near future posting. We took some photos with cell phone, but the quality is lacking, so check back in a few weeks for more.

Phone photo! Shows how one of the phototransistors is hidden in a Lifelike signal box. I have yet to disguise the infrared LED across the track from here.  Note the use of cinders as well as the mainline ballast mix. The mainline ballast is a mix of Buff, Gray and Iron Ore medium ballast from Woodland Scenics. This is a crushed walnut shell material which is light weight and tends to float if too much wet water or ballast cement is poured on. We are making our own ballast cement by mixing about  1 part white glue to 8-10 parts water with 6-8 drops of Dawn dishwashing detergent added to help it flow. We first wet the ballast with “wet water” water with a few drops of dishwashing detergent added. The glue is dribbled down the center of the track and allowed to wick through to the shoulder.

One more view of the Xing and a view of the country store which can be seen to the left of the upper photo. This is an MTH pre-built  building that Noah partly disassembled a then painted the details and weathered.

We had on hand a Plasticville “Hobo Jungle” which Scott painted and weathered. It seemed that the area below the steel trestle was a suitable location for a hobo camp, so while working on the scene discussed above we also prepared the site and added the Plasticville structures, Woodland Scenics Hobo figures and lots of tress and underbrush.

The scene turned out pretty good for using some cheap buildings. We intend to replace the campfire which came with the figures with an animated campfire for a little more interest. There are also more details to add, but only so much can be done while the glue is drying.

For Scott the big addition to the layout is the “Redler” coaling plant. Refer to page 13 and you can see the tower and base were built on the workbench. First the base needed to be set into the tabletop. It was carefully lined up with the existing yard tracks. The rail was cut and the ties removed. The cork and ballast were then scraped away to allow the “outriggers” to sit flush on the tabletop and the area for the dump was outline to be cut away.

The hole for the dump was cut out with a saber saw. The outline was pretty close but some filing and sanding were required to get everything lined up properly.

Below; the coal tower base is in place and ready to be ballasted in.

Three phone pics of the tower in place and a loco in for some service. The sand tower is not correct for the steam  era, but a similar one was erected in 1957 with the coming of the RS-11’s. It was provided by Jeff MacDonald. It looks nice, so we’re using it. There is another like it for West Virginia. I need to add a sand bin and numerous yard buildings. We’ve begun adding dirt, grass and other ground covers to the basic cinder ballast. Photos show an overgrown yard in the 50’s. The track was cleaned and we had a mini operating session. The rolling stock in the photos were scattered around the layout so using Loco 1984 the cars were rounded up, brought to West Duluth and sorted. It was a fun exercise and help to pin point areas of the track that needed attention. Many of the switches needed a good cleaning after all the ballasting.  The loco, 1984 is described on page 13. Using the sound functions whistle signals were given during maneuvers. Sound really adds a whole new dimension to model railroading. Moving the Weaver 2-8-0 that has no sound was just not as much fun! I brought it back to the shop for sound installation.
