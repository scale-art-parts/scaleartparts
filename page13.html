As part of my services offered I do a limited number of locomotive conversions, either adding DCC or going whole hog and installing the components for “Dead Rail” operation. The process either way involves initial testing running of the models to look for binds, shorts or other issues that affect the install and performance of the locomotive. For this I need a test track  with DC, DCC and “Dead Rail” capabilities. Concurrent with my move to Texas I decided to push the concept of my test track a little and have some fun with it. I have some wall space in my shop above some work surfaces which will accommodate a shallow, but fairly long layout. Looking at the plan below you will see the result of my thinking; a simple point to point about 22’ L by 1’ deep. I’m incorporating both a hand-built stub switch and a San Juan #6 point switch. I am working with guys that have one or the other or both. There is a slight grade and the big curve is 42” R. All these things are elements to help duplicate “real world” features a locomotive must negotiate and not just a straight, flat piece of track for testing. I decided to handlay the track since I have a lot of code 83 rail on hand. I have to transition from code 83 to code 100 for the San Juan switch. There will be some scenery and a few buildings. I designed the plan to accommodate Banta’s Cimarron Mine as well as Classic Miniature’s Grand Central Mine. I expect to have a lot of fun building and finishing this little project, though small it will provide many opportunities to detail and finish scenes; my favorite part of the hobby.

Here is an over all view looking toward the big curve.

You can see the work surfaces underneath. The base track level is 62” above the floor and the top of the grade is 66”. It provides a nice eye level view and keeps my work surfaces accessible. I use non-beveled Homabed from Cascade Rail Supply as roadbed with 3/4” plywood subroad bed. The benchwork is inexpensive 1X3. As you can see, the end closest to the camera widens a bit to 15”. When this photo was taken most of the ties are already in place.  

I made a simple jig to make tie strips for placement on the layout. It is a scrap of 3/4” plywood with basswood spacers. The spacers are thinner than the ties so the ties stand proud of the jig. All of the ties are cut from Mt. Albert O scale 6 X 8 strip wood. I cut them to a scale 6’ for my short line. Tie are pre-stained with leather dye diluted in Denatured alcohol. I first stained them with a light stain of USMC Black then stained random amounts in various tints of Medium Brown, gradually darkening the stain. The results is subtly non-uniform coloration, which I like. I did not distress the ties at this point.

Once all the ties are placed in the jig, (my jig makes a line of ties 18” long), a strip of 1/2” wide masking tape is pressed down onto the ties. I like the blue painter’s tape as it does not leave residue on the ties.

Slowly pulling up from both ends yields a ready to glue strip of evenly placed ties. Easy to do and if a little irregularity  slips in while gluing the strips, all the better. I like things a little less than perfect, it’s more like the real world.
